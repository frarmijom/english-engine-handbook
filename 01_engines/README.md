**Version:** 0.1  
**Level:** B1 → B2  
**Status:** Concept approved

---

## Main Question

> **How does English classify the world?**

English does not begin by deciding whether a noun takes **-s**.

It first decides how the thing is perceived:

> **Is it a unit or a mass?**

---

## Cognitive Objective

After studying this engine, the learner should automatically ask:

> **Is English seeing this as a UNIT or as a MASS?**

This question helps explain:

- Countable vs. uncountable nouns
- Singular vs. plural forms
- much vs. many
- a/an
- piece of...

## How the English Brain Thinks

A native speaker does not usually think:

> Can I add **-s**?

The deeper question is:

> **Am I talking about individual objects or about content?**

That decision happens almost automatically.

---

## Core Concept

### Countable = Units

These exist as individual units.

Examples:

- one server / three servers
- one error / several errors
- one ticket / many tickets
- one log / five logs
- one document / two documents

### Uncountable = Mass or Concept

These are perceived as a whole.

Examples:

- information
- advice
- software
- documentation
- traffic
- knowledge
- experience

They do not naturally divide into units.

```text
LOGS

📄   📄   📄

```

Logs can be counted.

---

## Counting a Mass

When English needs to count something uncountable, it creates a unit.

| Mass | Unit |
|---|---|
| water | a glass of water |
| information | a piece of information |
| advice | a piece of advice |
| bread | a slice of bread |
| equipment | a piece of equipment |
| furniture | a piece of furniture |
| software | a software license / a program |

## Words That Can Change

Some words can be countable or uncountable depending on meaning.

| Uncountable | Meaning | Countable | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| experience | professional knowledge | experiences | life events |
| coffee | drink in general | coffees | cups of coffee |
| paper | material | papers | academic documents |
| chicken | meat | chickens | animals |
| glass | material | glasses | cups / eyewear |

---

## Spanish Interference

Spanish often divides reality differently.

Spanish allows forms like:

```text
informaciones
consejos
equipos
```

English usually treats these as mass nouns in many professional contexts:

```text
information
advice
equipment
```

The learner must stop asking:

> How do I translate this from Spanish?

And start asking:

> How does English see this noun?

---

## Engine Law

> **English counts objects. It does not count concepts unless it creates a unit.**
